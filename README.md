## _What do we think when we listen to music?_

"From disco floors to deep space, jazz to jellyfish, shadows to spinning colours; looking at participants’ free-response descriptions eye-to-eye highlights the multi-faceted nature of music-influenced mental content (MIMC). 

  Extending beyond traditional isolated examinations of VMI, memories, and narratives, our open and unified probing of mental content—combined with mixed-method analyses—captures structural relations between thought types that have remained largely uncharted.   

  Human‑driven qualitative reading can attend to human-nuance that computational NLP approaches routinely flatten, enabling our analysis to identify the finer connective tissue between thought types. 

  The resulting thematic framework maps a comprehensive typography of listeners’ music influenced “thoughtscapes” (van der Walle et al., 2025), offering a more nuanced tapestry of imagination in action than previously captured by quantitative or computational approaches."

  —Hazel A. van der Walle [_Mar.2026_]

---

_Music‑Influenced Mental Content (MIMC)_ is defined as: 

Any mental content—visual imagery, verbal thoughts, autobiographical memories, imagined narratives, emotional appraisals, or multimodal scenes—that participants report during or immediately after listening to music and that they describe as influenced by, shaped by, or temporally associated with the music or listening context. 

MIMC includes content that participants explicitly attribute to the music, content they describe as being modulated or coloured by the music, and content that co‑occurs with listening without an explicit causal claim.

---

Rigorous, mixed-method qualitative analysis was conducted on 14,099 MIMC descriptions, involving: 
- multiple coding passes,
- multi-coder reliability checks,
- thematic analysis, and
- co-occurrence network analysis.

The resulting thematic framework comprehensively maps the “thoughtscapes” influenced by music and highlights the multi-faceted nature of MIMC. 

My hope is that these insights can lay the groundwork for future MIMC research considering the full spectrum and typology of listeners’ imaginative experiences.
